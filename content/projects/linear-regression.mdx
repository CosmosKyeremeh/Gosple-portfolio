Most introductions treat linear regression as curve-fitting. Viewed through linear algebra, it is something more precise: **a projection**.

## The Setup

Given $n$ observations, we seek $\hat{\beta}$ such that:

$$\mathbf{y} \approx \mathbf{X}\hat{\beta}$$

The column space $\text{col}(\mathbf{X})$ is a $p$-dimensional subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$. In general, $\mathbf{y} \notin \text{col}(\mathbf{X})$.

## The Geometric Question

Least squares asks: **what point in $\text{col}(\mathbf{X})$ is closest to $\mathbf{y}$?**

The answer is the orthogonal projection:

$$\hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{y}, \quad \mathbf{P} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^T$$

## Deriving the Normal Equations

The residual $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\hat{\beta}$ must be orthogonal to every column of $\mathbf{X}$:

$$\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{0}
\implies \mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X}\hat{\beta}$$

When $\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X}$ is invertible:

$$\hat{\beta} = (\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{y}$$

## The Pythagorean Decomposition

Because $\hat{\mathbf{y}} \perp \mathbf{e}$:

$$\|\mathbf{y}\|^2 = \|\hat{\mathbf{y}}\|^2 + \|\mathbf{e}\|^2$$

This is the Pythagorean theorem in $\mathbb{R}^n$. The $R^2$ statistic is the squared cosine of the angle between $\mathbf{y}$ and $\hat{\mathbf{y}}$.

## Why Collinearity Breaks OLS

When columns of $\mathbf{X}$ are linearly dependent, $\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X}$ is singular — the projection is undefined. Ridge regression adds $\lambda\mathbf{I}$ to force invertibility, trading bias for a stable solution.
